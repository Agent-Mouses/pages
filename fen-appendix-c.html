2. The Faucet: Visa Retrogression Controls Who Enters

Source: U.S. Department of State, Report of the Visa Office, Table V − Table VI, FY2008–2023.

Three phases:
• 2008–2015 (gray dominant): Retrogression = faucet OFF. Few new nurses can enter. Only those already in the US clear backlog (AOS). Group C is small → penalty undetectable.
• 2016–2019 (blue dominant, 99% in 2019): Visa current = faucet ON. Direct recruitment from Manila at full speed. Group C grows → penalty = −19.1%.
• 2022–2023 (blue ~90%): Post-COVID surge. Steady state, pipeline fully open.

Key insight: Contract-bound nurses (Group C) earn the prevailing wage — a legal floor set by DOL. They cannot negotiate higher because leaving = $20-25K penalty. This is where the 19.1% penalty comes from. It's contractual, not immigration-status-based. Even AOS nurses with EADs remain bound by their private contracts.

5. Supporting: TES Wage Premium Over Time (Table C1)

BLS OES: NAICS 561320 (Temporary Help Services) RN wage vs. All-Industry RN wage. Source: Bureau of Labor Statistics, 2008–2023.

Reading: In 8 of 16 years (2015–2021), travel nurses' taxable wages were BELOW the all-industry mean. Only during COVID (2022–23) did they show a large premium (+18%). From 2008–2014, the premium was small (+0.3% to +4.6%) and shrinking.

6. Supporting: Wanderly Compensation Data (Table C2)

Travel nurse job postings: taxable base pay vs. total compensation (incl. stipends) vs. BLS all-industry RN mean. Source: Wanderly platform data 2017–2023; BLS OES.

Reading: The blue line (taxable base) is what ACS/BLS would see — consistently BELOW the all-industry mean. The green line (total package including stipends) is much higher but invisible to surveys. This confirms travel nurses cannot inflate our benchmark.
